\begin{problem}
A very long cylinder, of radius $a$, carries a uniform polarization $\bv{P}$ perpendicular to its axis. Find the electric field inside the cylinder. Show that
the field outside the cylinder can be expressed in the form
$$\bv{E}(\bv{r})=\frac{a^2}{2\epsilon_0s^2}\left[2(\bv{P}\cdot\uv{s})\uv{s}-\bv{P}\right]$$
\end{problem}
\begin{soln}


  % Since $\bv{P}$ is uniform, $\rho_b=\bv{\nabla}\cdot{\bv{P}}=0$ and since it is perpendicular to the long axis of the cylinder $\sigma_b=\bv{P}\cdot\uv{n}=\bv{P}\cdot\uv{s}=\abs{\bv{P}}\cos\theta$.
  % So we can treat this as, like is done in the text, two cylinders slightly displaced from the original, one positively charged and the other negatively charged. The electric field inside one of
  % these cylinders is given by Gauss' law as
  % $$\int \bv{E}\cdot d\bv{a}=\frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}$$
  % where here since we are dealing with single, uniformly charged cylinders of charge density $\rho$ we can extract the field, which
  % $$\abs{\bv{E}}2\pi sL=\frac{\rho\pi s^2L}{\epsilon_0}\implies \bv{E}=\frac{\rho}{2\epsilon_0}\bv{s}$$
  % where $\bv{s}$ is the direction vector offset as the cylinders are and the radius of the Gaussian surface.
  % We can superimpose the fields for the positive and negative cylinders then to obtain that the total field is
  % $$\bv{E}=\frac{\rho}{2\epsilon_0}\bv{s}_+-\frac{\rho}{2\epsilon_0}\bv{s}_-=\frac{\rho}{2\epsilon_0}(\bv{s}_+-\bv{s}_-).$$
  % $\bv{s}_+-\bv{s}_-$ will cancel out any component other than the one indicating the separation between the two cylinders, pointing
  % from the $+$ cylinder to the $-$. This is the reverse of the direction of the polarization, which we defined as the dipole moment per unit volume, i.e.
  % the total dipole moment of a slice of the cylinder of length $L$ is $\bv{P}\pi a^2L$ or $-\pi a^2 L \rho(\bv{s}_+-\bv{s}_-)$ (note the negative in the second one,
  % the dipole displacement should point from $-$ to $+$, without the negative it points the other way)
  % so $\bv{P}= -\rho(\bv{s}_+-\bv{s}_-)$, as we'd expect for a single dipole, which this is if you squint. This simplifies the field to 
  % $$\bv{E}=\frac{-\bv{P}}{2\epsilon_0}.$$

  % Zero idea what to do about the outside field. Gauss' law again?

  We found the solution to Laplace's equation in cylindrical coordinates on the previous assignment so we'll reuse it here without derivation.
  Enforcing the first logical boundary condition we obtain two solutions due to the fact that $V(s,\phi)$ must remain finite at infinity
  and at the origin:
  $$V_{i}(s,\phi)=A_{i}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}s^n\left[A_n\sin(n\phi)+B_n\cos(n\phi)\right]$$
  and
  $$V_{o}(s,\phi)=A_o+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}s^{-n}\left[C_n\sin(n\phi)+D_n\cos(n\phi)\right].$$
  Next we require that the potential be continuous at the surface of the cylinder, $V_i(a,\phi)=V_o(a,\phi)$,
  $$A_o+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a^{-n}\left[C_n\sin(n\phi)+D_n\cos(n\phi)\right]=A_{i}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a^n\left[A_n\sin(n\phi)+B_n\cos(n\phi)\right]$$
  which tells, just by matching coefficients of the constants and the trig terms, says that
  \begin{align*}
    A_i & =A_o=A_0    \\
    C_n & =A_na^{2n}  \\
    D_n & =B_na^{2n}.
  \end{align*}
  so
  $$V_i(s,\phi)=A_0+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}s^n\left[A_n\sin(n\phi)+B_n\cos(n\phi)\right]$$
  and
  $$V_{o}(s,\phi)=A_0+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}s^{-n}\left[A_na^{2n}\sin(n\phi)+B_na^{2n}\cos(n\phi)\right].$$
  Next we require that
  $$\eval{(\bv{E}_o-\bv{E}_i)}_{a}\cdot\uv{s}=\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}=\frac{\sigma_b}{\epsilon_0}=\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}\cos\phi}{\epsilon_0}$$
  where $\sigma=\sigma_b$ because the cylinder is not charged. Our electric fields (dotted with $\uv{s}$ so we don't have to do more work) are
  $$\bv{E}_i\cdot\uv{s}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}-ns^{n-1}\left[A_n\sin(n\phi)+B_n\cos(n\phi)\right]$$
  and
  $$\bv{E}_o\cdot\uv{s}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}ns^{-n-1}\left[A_na^{2n}\sin(n\phi)+B_na^{2n}\cos(n\phi)\right]$$
  which when evaluated at $a$ and plugged into our boundary condition gives
  $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}na^{-n-1}\left[A_na^{2n}\sin(n\phi)+B_na^{2n}\cos(n\phi)\right]+na^{n-1}\left[A_n\sin(n\phi)+B_n\cos(n\phi)\right]=\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}\cos\phi}{\epsilon_0}$$
  which works out to
  $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[2A_nna^{n-1}\sin(n\phi)+2B_nna^{n-1}\cos(n\phi)\right]=\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}\cos\phi}{\epsilon_0}$$
  from which we can, again by matching coefficients, see that $A_n=0\forall n$ and $B_n=0\forall n\neq 1$.
  When $n=1$ we get that
  $$2B_1\cos(\phi)=\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}\cos\phi}{\epsilon_0}\implies B_1=\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}}{2\epsilon_0}$$
  which, from our previous expression for $D_n$, gives
  $$D_1=\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}a^{2}}{2\epsilon_0}$$
  so we obtain an expression for the potential, after much suffering,
  $$V(s,\phi)=\begin{cases}
      \displaystyle \frac{\abs{\bv{P}}}{2\epsilon_0}s\cos\phi    & s<a \\
      \displaystyle \frac{\abs{\bv{P}}a^2}{2s\epsilon_0}\cos\phi & s>a
    \end{cases}$$
  and so we obtain, through $\bv{E}=-\nabla V$, and noting that $\ux=\cos\phi\uv{s}-\sin\phi\uv{\phi}$,
  $$
    \bv{E}(s,\phi)=\begin{cases}
      \displaystyle -(\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}}{2\epsilon_0}\cos\phi\uv{s}-\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}}{2\epsilon_0}\sin\phi\uv{\phi})=-\frac{\abs{\bv{P}}}{2\epsilon_0}\ux & s<a \\
      \displaystyle \frac{a^2}{2s^2\epsilon_0}\left[\abs{\bv{P}}\cos(\phi)\uv{s}+\abs{\bv{P}}\sin(\phi)\uv{\phi}\right]                                      & s>a
    \end{cases}
  $$
  Since no explicit direction for $\bv{P}$ is given we'll say it's along $\ux$. This means that it is oriented along $\phi=0$,
  and we can write it using the coordinate transforms given in the back of Griffiths as
  $$\bv{P}=\abs{\bv{P}}\cos(\phi)\uv{s}-\abs{\bv{P}}\sin(\phi)\uv{\phi}.$$
  It is clear to see now that we can write the electric field outside as given in the problem statement as
  $$\left[2(\bv{P}\cdot\uv{s})\uv{s}-\bv{P}\right]=(2\abs{\bv{P}}\cos(\phi)\uv{s})-\abs{\bv{P}}\cos(\phi)\uv{s}+\abs{\bv{P}}\sin(\phi)\uv{\phi}=\abs{\bv{P}}\cos(\phi)\uv{s}+\abs{\bv{P}}\sin(\phi)\uv{\phi}.$$

\end{soln}
